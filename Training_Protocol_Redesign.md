# Evidence-Based Weekly Training Plan — 39-Year-Old Male on Medically Supervised TRT

## TL;DR
- The plan is sound in structure and, with the volume corrections below, will serve his priority stack (longevity → muscle → joints → VO2max → mobility): a 3.5-day upper/lower-biased split with every muscle trained 2×/week (~10–20 direct sets/muscle/week), one Norwegian 4×4 VO2max session, two 90-minute Zone 2 sessions on non-leg days, integrated Heavy Slow Resistance (HSR) knee rehab, and one full rest day.
- His two biggest assumption errors to fix: (1) he likely UNDER-counts effective volume for small muscles (arms, calves, delts, tibialis) and OVER-relies on compound "indirect" sets — the 2025 Pelland/Robinson meta-regression shows synergist sets count ~half, so direct isolation work is needed; (2) fragmenting Zone 2 offers no benefit and his 2×90 preference is fully evidence-supported — total accumulated volume drives adaptation, so keeping it whole on non-leg days is correct.
- The interference effect for hypertrophy and maximal strength is negligible when cardio and lifting are separated (ideally ≥3 h or on different days); only explosive/power is mildly attenuated (SMD −0.28). In compressed weeks, protect strength+rehab and the single 4×4; Zone 2 is the most flexible.

## Key Findings
1. **Volume**: Muscle-specific *direct* set volume is the primary hypertrophy driver; ~10–20 sets/muscle/week is the effective range with pronounced diminishing returns beyond ~12–20 (Pelland et al. 2025; Schoenfeld et al. 2017). **Strength: strong/RCT+meta-analysis.**
2. **Frequency**: 2×/week per muscle is optimal for coverage; frequency adds little to hypertrophy once volume is equated, but modestly benefits strength (Schoenfeld/Grgic/Krieger 2019; Pelland 2025). **Strong.**
3. **Proximity to failure**: 0–3 RIR is sufficient; going to momentary failure yields only a trivial hypertrophy advantage (ES ~0.19) and costs recovery (Refalo et al. 2023). **Strong.**
4. **VO2max**: The Norwegian 4×4 (Helgerud) is the best-validated single protocol, and cardiorespiratory fitness is the strongest exercise-related predictor of all-cause mortality (Helgerud 2007; Mandsager 2018). **Strong.**
5. **Zone 2**: Adaptations track total accumulated volume; splitting into micro-sessions adds nothing (Murphy et al. 2019). 2×90 min is ideal and knee-friendly. **Moderate–strong.**
6. **Knee rehab**: HSR has the strongest tendon evidence (Kongsgaard 2009/2010); isometric analgesia is variable/uncertain; the 24-hour pain-monitoring rule governs progression. **HSR: strong; isometric analgesia: weak/inconsistent.**
7. **Protein**: 1.6–2.2 g/kg/day (Morton 2018); TRT amplifies the anabolic ceiling but does not change protein needs. **Strong.**
8. **Supplements**: Creatine (strong), collagen+vitamin C pre-load (moderate/mechanistic), omega-3 and vitamin-D-if-deficient (moderate).

## Details

### 1. Challenging His Assumptions

**(a) Weekly volume per muscle.** The definitive current source is Pelland, Remmert, Robinson, Hinson & Zourdos, *Sports Medicine* (2025), "The Resistance Training Dose Response," 67 studies / 2058 participants. Key finding: the posterior probability that more volume increases both hypertrophy and strength was 100%, but with diminishing returns (strength plateauing considerably faster than hypertrophy). Critically, they validated a "fractional" method: an indirect/synergist set counts as **0.5**, a direct set as **1.0**. This is the single most important correction to his mental model. If he counts triceps work from pressing, or quads from leg press, as "full" sets, he is over-estimating effective volume for those muscles. Their model estimated ~0.24% hypertrophy gain per additional set at an average fractional volume of ~12 sets — comparable to Schoenfeld et al. (2017), who reported ~0.38%/set. Practical landmarks (also consistent with Israetel's MEV/MAV/MRV framework): ~10 sets/week is a solid minimum for growth, 12–20 the productive band, with substantial diminishing returns past ~20 direct sets/muscle/week. The plan below targets ~12–18 direct sets for large muscles and ~8–12 for small muscles, all at 2× frequency. **Evidence: strong (meta-regression).**

**(b) Exercise sequencing.** Compounds first (fatigue management, load specificity). Rehab isometrics can be placed pre-session as a warm-up/analgesic primer or as a separate stimulus; HSR itself is a main lift. Cardio-vs-lifting order: the interference effect is smaller than gym folklore suggests. Schumann et al., *Sports Medicine* (2022), 43 studies, reported standardized mean differences of **−0.06 (95% CI −0.20 to 0.09; p=0.446) for maximal strength**, **−0.01 for hypertrophy (ns)**, and **−0.28 (95% CI −0.48 to −0.08; p=0.007) for explosive strength** — and even that power decrement was more pronounced in the same session and non-significant when sessions were separated by ≥3 h. Because this plan puts Zone 2 and the 4×4 on non-lifting days, interference is essentially a non-issue. **Evidence: strong (meta-analysis).**

**(c) Recovery adequacy.** Every-muscle-2× with one rest day and cardio on separate days is well within recoverable limits. Protein 1.6–2.2 g/kg/day (Morton et al., *BJSM* 2018; plateau ~1.62 g/kg, confidence interval extending to 2.2). Sleep 7–9 h. Deload every ~6–8 weeks. TRT modestly accelerates recovery capacity but requires medical monitoring (Section 8). **Evidence: strong.**

The "3.5-day" split = 3 full lower/upper days + a half upper day on Saturday. Every muscle is trained twice: Lower A/Lower B cover quads/hams/glutes/calves/tib across the week via overlap; Upper A/Upper B cover chest/back/delts/arms twice.

### 3. Day-by-Day Detail

**MONDAY — Lower A (quad-biased) + HSR knee rehab**
- *Warm-up (10–12 min)*: 5 min bike; leg swings, ankle dorsiflexion mobilization (knee-to-wall), hip airplanes; Spanish-squat isometric 3×30–45 s at ~70° knee flexion as an analgesic primer + tendon load (analgesia evidence is uncertain — used as a tolerable warm-up and strain stimulus, not a cure).
- *Main lifts*:
  1. **Leg press (HSR component)** — 4×6–8, 3-0-3 tempo, 2 RIR, 3 min rest. Primary heavy-slow tendon loader (Kongsgaard protocol progresses 15RM→6RM over 12 weeks).
  2. **Hack squat or pendulum squat** — 3×8–10, 2 RIR, 2–3 min. Machine chosen for controlled, knee-friendly loading.
- *Accessories*:
  3. Leg extension (single-leg, left emphasis) — 3×10–12, 2-s squeeze, 1 RIR (direct quad + rehab).
  4. Adductor machine — 3×12–15.
  5. Standing calf raise (gastrocnemius) — 4×8–12, 2-s stretched pause.
  6. Tibialis raise — 3×15–20.
  7. Copenhagen plank progression (adductor/hip) — 2×15–30 s/side.
  8. Lying/seated leg curl (light, to give hamstrings a 2nd weekly exposure) — 2×10–12.
- *Knee stabilizer/single-leg*: slow eccentric step-downs 2×8/side (pain ≤3/10, 24-h rule).

**TUESDAY — Zone 2 (90 min)**
- Modality: upright/recumbent bike or incline treadmill walk (cycling preferred to spare the patellar tendon between rehab days).
- Intensity: at/just below LT1 (~1.7–2.0 mmol/L lactate; ~65–75% HRmax; able to speak full sentences — talk test positive).
- Structure: one continuous 90-min block. Murphy, Lahart, Carlin & Murtagh, *Sports Medicine* (2019) meta-analysis (19 trials, 1080 participants, volume/intensity/mode matched): "There were no differences between accumulated and continuous groups for any cardiorespiratory fitness or blood pressure outcomes" — confirming his 2×90 preference is optimal and that fragmenting adds nothing for aerobic fitness.

**WEDNESDAY — Upper A (push-biased)**
- *Warm-up*: 5 min row/bike; band shoulder dislocates; scap push-ups; cuff activation.
- *Main*:
  1. Machine chest press or barbell bench — 4×6–8, 2 RIR, 2–3 min.
  2. Standing overhead press (machine or barbell) — 3×8–10, 2 RIR.
- *Accessories*:
  3. Incline DB press — 3×10–12, 1–2 RIR.
  4. Cable lateral raise — 4×12–20, 0–1 RIR (delts need direct volume).
  5. Triceps pushdown — 3×10–15.
  6. Overhead cable triceps extension — 2×12–15 (long head).
  7. Rear-delt / reverse pec-deck — 3×15–20.
  8. Incline DB curl (1 direct set to give biceps its 2nd weekly exposure) — 2×10–12.
- *Core*: cable crunch 3×12–15; suitcase carry 2×30 m/side (grip + core).

**THURSDAY — Norwegian 4×4 (Helgerud protocol)**
- *Warm-up*: 10 min at 60–70% HRmax.
- *Main set*: 4 intervals × 4 min at **90–95% HRmax**, each followed by **3 min active recovery** at 60–70% HRmax.
- *Cool-down*: 5 min easy. Total ~38–40 min.
- Modality: bike, ski-erg, or incline treadmill — pick the lowest-impact/knee-friendly option (bike or ski-erg ideal).
- Evidence: Helgerud et al., *Med Sci Sports Exerc* 2007;39(4):665–671 (40 moderately-trained men, 3×/week for 8 weeks) — the 4×4 group improved VO2max by **7.2%**, versus 5.5% (15/15 intervals), 2.0% (lactate-threshold training) and −0.6% (long slow distance). **Evidence: strong (RCT).**

**FRIDAY — Lower B (hinge/posterior chain) + HSR**
- *Warm-up*: as Monday; add isometric hamstring bridge holds.
- *Main*:
  1. **Romanian deadlift or hip-hinge machine** — 4×6–8, controlled tempo, 2 RIR. Posterior chain + hamstring HSR.
  2. **Seated/lying leg curl** — 4×8–12, 1–2 RIR (direct hamstring; important because RDL under-loads the distal hamstring).
- *Accessories*:
  3. Bulgarian split squat (elevated, upright torso; knee tracks over foot, ≤90° per pain) — 3×8–10/side (single-leg stability + quad/glute).
  4. Hip thrust — 3×8–12 (glute max).
  5. Glute-medius: cable hip abduction or machine — 3×15–20 (key knee-stabilizer for patellofemoral tracking, often neglected).
  6. Seated calf raise (soleus) — 4×10–15.
  7. Tibialis + ankle mobility circuit.
  8. Leg extension (light, quad 2nd exposure balance) — 2×12–15.

**SATURDAY — Upper B (pull-biased) [the 0.5 day] + Zone 2 (90 min)**
- Do Upper B strength first while fresh; Zone 2 later in the day (≥3 h separation) or as its own session.
- *Upper B*:
  1. Weighted/assisted pull-up or lat pulldown — 4×6–10, 2 RIR.
  2. Chest-supported row — 3×8–12, 1–2 RIR.
  3. Cable row (neutral grip) — 3×10–12.
  4. Face pull — 3×15–20 (rear delt/cuff).
  5. Biceps: incline DB curl — 3×8–12; cable curl — 2×12–15.
  6. Forearms: wrist curls + reverse curls — 2–3×12–20 (grip strength is a mortality proxy).
  7. Rear delt + shrug (trap) — 2–3×12–15.
  8. Triceps pushdown (1 set, 2nd weekly exposure) — 2×12–15.
- *Zone 2*: 90 min as Tuesday.

**SUNDAY — Complete rest.** Optional gentle mobility/walking; no structured load.

### 5. Integrated Knee Rehabilitation (evidence-based)
- **HSR — strongest evidence.** Kongsgaard et al., *Scand J Med Sci Sports* 2009 and *Am J Sports Med* 2010: heavy slow resistance (leg press, squat, hack squat), 3–4 sets, progressing 15RM→6RM over 12 weeks, slow tempo (~3 s concentric/3 s eccentric). HSR matched eccentric decline squats and beat corticosteroid injection at long-term follow-up, and drove favorable tendon remodeling. Built into Mon/Fri main lifts. **Strong (RCT).**
- **Isometrics / Spanish squat — honest appraisal.** Rio et al. 2015 (n=6 volleyball players) reported dramatic immediate analgesia (5×45 s at 70% MVIC); however, this has NOT reliably replicated — Rio et al. 2017 (in-season RCT) found isometrics only modestly better than isotonic, and later studies show variable or no immediate analgesia. Verdict: use Spanish-squat isometrics (3–5×30–45 s at ~70°) as a tolerable warm-up primer and tendon-loading tool; do NOT rely on them as guaranteed pain relief. The load itself is beneficial regardless of the analgesic response. **Weak/inconsistent for analgesia; sound as loading.**
- **Supporting musculature**: quads (leg ext/press), hamstrings (curls/RDL), glute max (thrust), glute medius (abduction), adductors (Copenhagen/machine), tibialis (raises), calf/soleus (raises), single-leg stability (step-downs, split squats), ankle dorsiflexion mobility (knee-to-wall).
- **24-hour pain-monitoring rule (Silbernagel/Cook model)**: pain during loading up to ~3–5/10 is acceptable; the deciding criterion is that pain returns to baseline within 24 h and does not progressively worsen morning-to-morning. The left knee (more symptomatic) gets slightly reduced load/volume and unilateral emphasis until symmetric. **Moderate (expert consensus + cohort).**

### 6. Conditioning Protocols (recap + rationale)
- **Norwegian 4×4** (Thursday): 4×4 min @ 90–95% HRmax / 3 min active recovery, on its own day ≥48 h from heavy lower days — interference negligible, recovery preserved. *Why:* highest-yield single VO2max stimulus.
- **Zone 2** (Tue/Sat, 2×90 min): LT1/first ventilatory threshold, ~1.7–2.0 mmol/L, talk-test positive; cycling primary (knee-sparing). Total 180 min/week, kept as 2×90 because accumulated volume — not session count — drives adaptation (Murphy 2019). *Why:* aerobic base underpins VO2max and metabolic health without adding tendon load on rehab days.

### 7. 12-Week Progression & Deload
- **Weeks 1–3**: establish baselines; RIR 2–3; HSR at ~12–15RM loads; conservative knee volume; confirm 24-h pain rule tolerance.
- **Weeks 4–6**: add ~1–2 sets to lagging muscles; HSR progresses toward 8–10RM; increase 4×4 workload to keep HR at 92–95%; RIR 1–2 on isolations.
- **Week 7 (deload)**: ~50% volume, RIR 3–4, moderate intensity; keep one easy 4×4-style session and reduced Zone 2.
- **Weeks 8–11**: push toward the top of the volume band (up to ~18–20 direct sets for priority muscles); HSR to 6RM; RIR 0–2 on isolations, 1–2 on compounds.
- **Week 12 (deload/retest)**: reduce volume; retest VO2max proxy (4×4 pace/HR), key lifts, VISA-P knee score, limb girths.
- Deload every 6–8 weeks or sooner if performance, sleep, or joint symptoms regress.

### 8. Recovery & TRT-Specific Considerations (physician-supervised)
- Protein 1.6–2.2 g/kg/day across 3–4 meals (~0.4 g/kg/meal).
- Sleep 7–9 h; manage life stress; ~48 h between heavy sessions for the same muscle.
- **TRT monitoring (his physician's domain)**: hematocrit (erythrocytosis is the most common adverse effect, greatest with IM injections; American guidelines flag ~50%, European ~54% — many clinicians hold/adjust at Hct >52–54% due to thrombotic risk), blood pressure, lipids, PSA + prostate exam, and estradiol as clinically indicated. Check at baseline, ~3–6 months, then annually. Because TRT raises hematocrit, and high-intensity work plus dehydration further concentrate blood, hydrate diligently around the 4×4.

### 9. Longevity / Preventive-Care Framing (ranking the modalities)
Attia's "Centenarian Decathlon" frames four pillars — stability, strength, Zone 2, VO2max — reverse-engineered from the functional tasks he wants in his "marginal decade." Ranked by strength of the all-cause-mortality evidence:

1. **VO2max / cardiorespiratory fitness — strongest.** Mandsager et al., *JAMA Netw Open* 2018;1(6):e183605 (122,007 patients): "cardiorespiratory fitness was inversely associated with all-cause mortality without an observed upper limit of benefit." Elite versus low fitness carried an adjusted hazard ratio of **0.20 (95% CI 0.16–0.24; P<.001)** — equivalently, low fitness versus elite HR **5.04 (95% CI 4.10–6.20)**, a gradient that rivals or exceeds major clinical risk factors like diabetes or smoking. This is why the single 4×4 is non-negotiable and VO2max ranks high for longevity despite being #4 on his stated priority list — it is the highest-yield longevity lever.
2. **Muscle strength / mass — strong.** Grip and muscular strength independently predict all-cause mortality across meta-analyses of ~2 million participants (e.g., ~31% lower mortality risk in the strongest vs. weakest; ~16% higher all-cause mortality per 5-kg grip decrease). For resistance training as an activity: Momma et al., *Br J Sports Med* 2022;56:755–763 found muscle-strengthening activity "associated with a 10–17% lower risk of all-cause mortality, CVD, total cancer, diabetes"; Shailendra et al., *Am J Prev Med* 2022;63:277–285 found any resistance training lowered all-cause mortality risk **15% (RR 0.85; 95% CI 0.77–0.93)**, CVD mortality 19%, and cancer mortality 14%.
3. **Zone 2 — supportive but mechanistically softer.** Drives mitochondrial density, capillarization, and metabolic health, and it underpins VO2max — but the "Zone 2 *specifically*" mortality evidence is less direct than for peak fitness; total aerobic volume is what matters most.
4. **Stability / mobility — expert-opinion/mechanistic** for fall/injury prevention and preserving function into later decades; integrated here via single-leg work, carries, and mobility drills.

*A note on dose:* Shailendra's dose-response showed the maximal all-cause mortality reduction (~27%) at roughly **60 min/week** of resistance training, following a J-shaped curve with diminished benefit at ≥150 min/week. His muscle-building volumes will exceed this mortality-optimal dose — an acceptable trade for his #2 goal (muscle gain), not a longevity liability, since the excess is about hypertrophy, not survival.

### 10. Supplements (connective tissue / joint / muscle; excluding TRT)
- **Creatine monohydrate, 3–5 g/day — STRONG (RCT + meta-analysis).** Reliable small-to-moderate gains in strength, lean mass, and training quality; ~99% absorbed; monohydrate is the proven form and no loading is required. *Why:* highest evidence-to-cost ratio supplement for his #2 goal, with a favorable safety profile.
- **Collagen/gelatin 15 g + ~50 mg vitamin C, taken ~60 min pre-loading — MODERATE/MECHANISTIC.** Shaw, Lee-Barthel, Ross, Wang & Baar, *Am J Clin Nutr* 2017;105(1):136–143 (n=8, RCT crossover): subjects who took 15 g gelatin 1 h before exercise showed **double** the amino-terminal propeptide of collagen I (a collagen-synthesis marker) versus placebo. The effect requires the mechanical-loading window, so take it before the Mon/Fri rehab/HSR sessions. Clinical outcome data are mixed/modest, but the mechanism is sound and downside minimal. *Why:* directly targets his tendon/joint goal.
- **Omega-3 (EPA/DHA ~2–3 g/day) — MODERATE.** Attenuates anabolic resistance and modestly supports strength/muscle (larger effect in older/deficient individuals; smaller in young, well-fed trainees); anti-inflammatory support for recovery.
- **Vitamin D3 800–2000 IU/day IF deficient — MODERATE, conditional.** Correcting a deficiency supports muscle function and type II fibers; supplementing when already replete adds little. Test the serum level first.
- **Not recommended as hypertrophy drivers**: HMB (neutral in well-fed trained lifters), exotic creatine forms (no proven superiority over monohydrate), and most multi-ingredient joint "cocktails."

## Recommendations
1. **Immediately re-audit per-muscle volume using the fractional method** (synergist = 0.5 set). Add direct isolation work for delts (lateral raises), arms, calves, tibialis, glute-medius, and hamstrings (curls) until each large muscle hits ~12–18 and each small muscle ~8–12 *direct* sets/week at 2× frequency. *Threshold to change:* if any muscle is <10 effective direct sets, add sets; if >20 with poor recovery or joint symptoms, cut.
2. **Keep Zone 2 as 2×90 min** on non-leg days — evidence supports accumulated volume; do not fragment it onto lifting days.
3. **Keep the single 4×4 on its own day**, ≥48 h from heavy lower sessions. Retest the VO2max proxy every 6–12 weeks; if it plateaus, progress interval workload (power/pace at target HR) before adding a second weekly session.
4. **Run knee rehab as integrated HSR** (Mon/Fri main lifts, 15RM→6RM over 12 weeks) governed by the 24-h pain rule; treat isometrics as a warm-up primer, not a cure.
5. **Nutrition:** protein 1.6–2.2 g/kg/day; creatine 3–5 g/day; collagen + vitamin C ~60 min before rehab days; omega-3 daily; vitamin D only if deficient.
6. **Compressed-week triage:** protect (a) the two lower-body strength+rehab sessions and (b) the single 4×4. First to cut is one Zone 2 session; next, trim strength *accessory* volume; drop a full strength day only as a last resort.
7. **TRT labs on schedule with his physician** (hematocrit, BP, lipids, PSA); hydrate around high-intensity sessions.

## Caveats
- Individual response varies; RIR and volume figures are starting points to auto-regulate by recovery, sleep, and joint symptoms.
- The isometric-analgesia evidence is weak and inconsistent — do not over-promise pain relief from Spanish squats.
- The Zone 2 "45–90 min minimum" is a practitioner heuristic, not a hard RCT threshold; the operative principle is total weekly aerobic volume. The Murphy (2019) meta-analysis also studied mostly sedentary/overweight adults doing moderate-intensity walking, so external validity to a trained lifter is reasonable but not exact.
- The "no packaging effect" conclusion applies to low-intensity/Zone 2 work, NOT to high-intensity intervals: for the 4×4, session structure DOES matter, so do not fragment interval work.
- Resistance-training mortality data are observational with a possible J/U-shape at very high volumes; the hypertrophy volumes here intentionally exceed the mortality-optimal ~60 min/week for the sake of his muscle-gain goal.
- All TRT decisions and lab thresholds belong to the treating physician; this plan does not adjust his hormone therapy.
- Supplement effects (except creatine) are modest; they supplement — never replace — adequate total protein and progressive loading.
